Frequently Asked Questions

Everything You Need to Know

Health insurance is a contract that protects your finances from the high cost of medical expenses. While you can technically skip it, doing so leaves you and your family vulnerable to financial hardship in the event of an unexpected illness or injury. It is a vital tool for risk management, not an optional expense.
An employer-provided plan is a great start, but it may not be enough. These plans often have limited coverage, may not cover your family comprehensively, and are tied to your employment. It’s wise to have a personal health insurance plan to ensure continuous and adequate coverage, regardless of your job.
Choosing the right plan involves a personalized assessment. It’s important to consider factors such as the sum insured, the network of hospitals, the policy's terms for pre-existing conditions, and your premium budget. A thorough comparison of these aspects will help you find a plan that fits your specific needs.
There is no one-size-fits-all answer. The ideal sum insured depends on your city of residence, your family's health history, and the rising cost of medical care. It is a personal decision that requires a forward-looking view to ensure your coverage remains adequate in the future.
Premiums paid for health insurance are generally eligible for tax deductions under Section 80D of the Income Tax Act. The specific limits and rules can vary, so it’s important to understand how these benefits apply to your personal tax situation.
Can I skip buying life insurance? Life insurance is a financial safety net for your dependents. Its primary purpose is to provide a lump sum to your family in the event of your unfortunate demise. If you have financial dependents, such as a spouse or children, skipping life insurance would leave them exposed to significant financial risk.
Your life insurance cover should be determined by your financial responsibilities. It needs to be enough to cover your dependents' living expenses, any outstanding loans, and future financial goals like your children's education. A careful assessment of these factors will help you arrive at the right amount.
The choice of policy depends on your primary objective. A term plan offers pure protection at a low cost, making it ideal for covering risk. Other policies like whole life or ULIPs combine insurance with a savings or investment component, each with its own benefits and limitations.
Guaranteed benefits are the amounts the insurer promises to pay regardless of market performance, such as the sum assured. Non-guaranteed benefits (e.g., bonuses, loyalty additions) are an extra benefit that may be paid out depending on the insurer's fund performance.
Claim rejections can occur, often due to non-disclosure of health information at the time of purchase. It is crucial to provide accurate information when applying for a policy. If a claim is rejected, you have the right to appeal the decision with the insurance company or approach the Insurance Ombudsman.
